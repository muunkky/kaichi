Architecture Decision

ADR-078 · Accepted 2026-07-02

Deck generation ships as a repo-local writer ⇄ reviewer skill pair: the agent writes each deck editorially, and a fresh-context review gates every one

Lifecycle Artifact Decks via a Repo-Local Writer ⇄ Reviewer Skill Pair — the decision record for Phase 1 of PRD-024, distilled from the design doc's full deliberation. The headlines alone carry the decision and what it asks of you.

Its deployment scope was later superseded-in-part by ADR-086 (2026-07-05): the pair now ships in the scaffold to every client. Every other decision recorded here — the editorial writer ⇄ reviewer contract, the visual system, the provenance stamping — stands unchanged.

ADR-078 · d56a0419 · 1/12

Situation · complication · question

PRD-024 demands decks that are LLM-written, judgment-reviewed, hermetic, and one considered system — constraints no template pipeline can meet

Situation. Lifecycle artifacts (PRDs, ADRs, and later design docs, roadmap branches, sprints) exist only as long-form markdown. Complication. The owner has no fast visual re-read of their own documents and no leadership-quality presentation form when an artifact needs sign-off from time-constrained senior reviewers. Question. How should Phase 1 deliver "one conversational request turns any PRD or ADR into a reviewed, self-contained, browser-openable, email-attachable deck"?

The PRD's binding constraints shape the architecture:

  • The generator must be the resident LLM agent, not a template pipeline — "LLM-quality ceiling, not template ceiling" (C2).
  • Quality criteria SC3–SC6 are reviewer judgments applied to every deck, never reducible to a checklist or rubric (C2 binds the per-deck gate).
  • Output must be hermetic and zero-tooling for recipients (C1/C3); the source document stays authoritative (C4); all decks must read as one considered visual system (G10).
ADR-078 · d56a0419 · 2/12

The decision

A repo-local skill pair — gitban-deck-writer ⇄ gitban-deck-reviewer — with no MCP tools, no Python pipeline, no hooks, and no gate framework

The resident LLM agent writes each deck editorially: structure, slide count, emphasis, headlines, and on-slide evidence are per-artifact judgments within per-type narrative arcs that are defaults, never slide skeletons. A fresh-context reviewer sub-agent applies PRD SC3–SC6 as judgments on every deck before it can be called done.

The key tradeoff: we accept non-deterministic generation gated by non-deterministic judgment — an LLM writer checked by an LLM reviewer, with the known risk of reviewer leniency drift — in exchange for escaping the template ceiling.

The editorial work is the generation: a deck's value is per-artifact judgment about what matters and how to argue it — exactly what a deterministic renderer cannot compute. Every tool-shaped delivery reintroduces the template ceiling C2 forbids.

Deployment-scope note. The "repo-local, outside the scaffold manifest" facet of this decision was later reversed by ADR-086 (2026-07-05): the pair is now promoted into the scaffold and shipped to every client via the deploy engine. The editorial writer ⇄ reviewer contract, the chrome system, the gate, and the provenance stamping decided here are unchanged.

ADR-078 · d56a0419 · 3/12

The shape of the delivery

The PRD forbids templating the content and demands system-izing the chrome — different layers, opposite treatments

  • Chrome: one curated embedded CSS system (assets/deck.css plus a documented per-slide HTML grammar). The writer composes freely within the grammar and never authors freeform CSS; the sole escape valve is inline SVG evidence constrained to the token palette.
  • Runtime: a scrolling Slidedoc of slide sections with ~60 lines of dependency-free, noscript-degradable navigation JS.
  • Identity: decks are committed artifacts at docs/decks/<source-basename>.html — one deck per source; regeneration overwrites in place; source renames (including NOM→ADR promotion) regenerate at the new basename and delete the orphan.
  • Provenance: source path, source-content commit (with +dirty / uncommitted honesty markers), mode, append-only steering history, and the review outcome transcribed from the reviewer's verdict — never writer-asserted — in an in-file HTML-comment block plus visible title/colophon stamping.
  • One advisory script (stdlib-only, always exit 0) scans for portability, hygiene-leak, and chrome-discipline defects; it informs iteration and can never gate.
ADR-078 · d56a0419 · 4/12

The gate

A deck is done only when a fresh-context reviewer says so: deck-first read, structured verdict, at most two revision rounds, failing closed

  • Deck-first read order — the headline and craft judgments are formed cold, before the reviewer opens the source; the source is read second, in full, for faithfulness.
  • Structured verdict — APPROVED / REVISE / REJECT, with a mandatory headline-story restatement and a named strongest doubt in every verdict, including APPROVED.
  • At most 2 revision rounds; on REJECT or rounds exhausted the loop fails closed — the owner receives a failure report and the draft is left uncommitted.
  • The review contract resists rubber-stamping structurally: a fresh context per round with the writer's rationale withheld, and the outcome transcribed into the artifact rather than asserted by the writer. Human SC6 blind spot-checks sit above the loop at launch.
ADR-078 · d56a0419 · 5/12

Positions on the design's open questions

Four questions are closed here: dirty decks commit with advice, permalinks default-suppress, steering resets conversationally, the reviewer inherits the session model

  1. Dirty-source decks are committable. The <sha>+dirty stamp and colophon statement make the state honest; the writer advises holding the commit until the source lands but does not block — blocking would make the deck less available exactly when the owner is iterating hardest. On APPROVED with a dirty stamp, the writer still commits per the loop contract and surfaces the hold-advice.
  2. Forge permalink: default SUPPRESS. "Public https forge URL" is undecidable at generation time and probing is forbidden; a wrong guess leaks a private permalink into an emailed file. Include only on a once-asked, recorded owner affirmation that the origin is public. SSH and unconfirmed https origins always suppress; a local path is never emitted.
  3. Steering history is append-only, with conversational reset. "Regenerate fresh" drops prior steering; no dedicated verb or mechanism ships. Revisit if steering histories accrete contradictions the conversation can't clear.
  4. The reviewer inherits the session model. No pinning to a strongest-model knob; the PRD's SC6 blind spot-checks arbitrate whether inherited-model review is sufficient, and a failed spot-check is the trigger to reconsider.

Alternatives considered — the tool-shaped deliveries

A deterministic renderer — MCP tool or Jinja pipeline — is the template ceiling PRD C2 rejects, however it is packaged

MCP tool (render_deck) / Python pipeline

A deterministic renderer is the template ceiling PRD C2 rejects; it also adds manifest/release coupling and an adopter-visible surface out of scope.

Extending the gitban/views/ Jinja pipeline

The same objection in existing-code form: it projects structured state into fixed layouts, and deck content has no data model to project.

Repo-local skill pair (chosen)

The agent writes editorially within a fixed chrome system; judgment gates every deck. No template ceiling — the decisive win. (Deployment scope later revisited by ADR-086.)

Alternatives considered — the looser and heavier options

Freeform CSS defeats the one-system goal, a vendored framework reads as a default, and sidecars or disposable decks break identity

AlternativeWhy it lost
Per-deck freeform CSSMaximal generator freedom, but per-generation taste variance defeats G10 on its face; every review would re-litigate typography.
Third-party deck framework (e.g., reveal.js inlined)Vendored dependency, ~10k lines per file, presenter-oriented, and reads as a framework default rather than a considered system.
Sidecar metadata filesDoubles the artifact count, can drift, breaks one-file-travels; the deck itself is the always-co-located metadata home.
Disposable (uncommitted) decksNo stable identity for regeneration, linking, or diffing; "the deck tracks the doc" becomes meaningless.
Freshness automation (staleness hooks/CI)Scoped out by the PRD; the stamp makes staleness detectable and regeneration is cheap. Future consideration, revisit with usage.

All alternatives were weighed in full in the design doc; this record distills them.

ADR-078 · d56a0419 · 8/12

Consequences, stated honestly

The honest bill: every deck costs a generation plus review, reviewer leniency drift is a standing risk, and committed decks can go stale

Positive.

  • No shipped-surface footprint at decision time — no manifest, package, hook, or scaffold changes; rollback is deleting two skill directories, docs/decks/, and one test file. (Deployment scope later revisited by ADR-086.)
  • "Done" carries evidence: the transcribed review outcome is an auditable per-deck record that judgment ran — without replacing the judgment.
  • Stable deck identity makes regeneration idempotent and the source⇄deck mapping bijective, with no registry or sidecar files.
  • Chrome consistency is a property of one versioned stylesheet, evolved by normal PRs; every deck benefits from each fix.

Negative.

  • Every deck costs a full generation plus at least one fresh-context review — including one-slide steering tweaks. Slower and costlier than a renderer.
  • Reviewer leniency drift is a standing risk that structure mitigates but cannot eliminate; the backstop is human spot-checking, which does not scale automatically.
  • Committed decks shared stale remain possible; detectability, not prevention, is the guarantee.
  • Regenerated decks produce wholesale, effectively unreviewable HTML diffs in PRs and grow the repo over time.
  • Skill-prose contracts are enforced by agent compliance plus one advisory script — weaker than code-enforced invariants, by explicit PRD design.

Neutral: repo-local only — adopters get nothing until a separate, later scaffold-packaging decision. This specific consequence was reversed by ADR-086, which made exactly that decision and promoted the pair into the scaffold. Phase 2 extends the same architecture, and nothing here forecloses it.

ADR-078 · d56a0419 · 9/12

Validation

The launch gate is PRD SC1–SC9 demonstrated on real artifacts — including the blind protocol and forced failure paths — with named revisit triggers

Launch gate (Phase B of the design): SC1–SC9 demonstrated on real artifacts from this repo — including the SC6 blind protocol (independent rater, not told the deck is generated, source withheld first) — plus forced REVISE and REJECT paths behaving per the loop contract, and the owner having used it on a document they actually care about (a named human-accepted criterion). Outcomes recorded in the design doc's revision history; per-deck review objects are the audit trail.

Revisit triggers:

  • An SC6 spot-check failure — reconsider reviewer model inheritance (question 4).
  • Steering histories accreting contradictions in practice (question 3).
  • Staleness causing real incidents — reopen freshness automation.
  • Phase 2 presenter mode — deck-identity variants.
  • Any scaffold-packaging proposal — a separate decision; the repo-local pair is what would be promoted, not bypassed. (This trigger has since fired: ADR-086 promoted the pair into the scaffold.)
